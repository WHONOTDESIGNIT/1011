## Introduction

He sent us a photo of a Philips IPL device and asked: “Can you produce this?”

We looked at it. It was an exact replica of a Philips model. We told him: “No, we do not produce copies of other brands.”

His response: “I don’t need the same energy. I just need it to look exactly the same and glow when I press the button.”

Let that sink in.

He did not care if the light actually removed hair. He did not care if the skin sensor worked, or existed at all. He just wanted a flashlight that looked like a Philips IPL device.

Why did he ask for this? Because he had already bought similar fakes from another supplier. Those devices sold well, for a while. Customers received shiny boxes with glowing lights that did absolutely nothing for their body hair.

But here is the kicker: his previous supplier was a tiny workshop that made unauthorized Philips replicas. Philips’ legal team found them. The supplier received a cease-and-desist letter and a lawsuit threat. They shut down overnight.

The buyer was now desperate to find someone else to produce the same fake. He was not looking for a partner. He was looking for a new victim.

The lesson, if there is one, is simple. If a buyer asks for “just the look, not the performance,” run. That brand will not last. And when it collapses, it may take your reputation with it.

## The Screen Can Lie Even When the Device Looks Real

We have seen buyers get fooled by fake replicas. But there is a subtler lie: energy numbers that do not exist.

Here is how it works. A supplier’s device actually delivers **10 J/cm²**, which is already high and potentially unsafe without cooling. But the supplier changes the display software to show **18 J/cm²** when tested. Then they write on the spec sheet and product page: “Maximum energy: 20 J/cm².”

From a distance, the numbers look impressive. **20 J/cm²** sounds better than the **5-6 J/cm²** used by reputable brands. Consumers who do not know the physics assume more energy means better results.

The reality is the opposite. **20 J/cm²** from an uncooled IPL device will cause second-degree burns on many skin types. Most home IPL power supplies cannot actually store that much energy because the capacitors are not designed for it. And even if the system tried to deliver it, cheap plastic housings cannot dissipate the resulting heat safely.

These fake numbers exist only on paper and on the display screen. In actual use, the device either underperforms or over-burns.

If you want to catch this, ask for independent third-party test reports rather than the supplier’s own documents. Confirm that energy was measured with a calibrated energy meter, not a software readout. And compare the claimed output against the power-supply capacity. Basic physics does not lie.

## The Most Dangerous Part Is Often the Adapter

The fake energy numbers live on the screen. But there is something even more dangerous, something you plug into the wall.

Most brand owners never think about the power adapter. That is exactly what dishonest suppliers count on.

There are three grades of power adapters in the IPL industry. Medical grade, certified to **IEC 60601**, is very safe and used by premium brands. Commercial certified, non-medical but still **UL/CE/FCC/ROHS** certified, is reasonably safe and used by most legitimate mid-tier brands. Then there is the unregulated tier: no certification, minimal testing, cheapest components. Very low safety. Very high danger.

The dangerous ones cost **$2-3** instead of **$8-12** for certified adapters. They skip overcurrent protection, thermal shutdown, and proper insulation.

What can happen? The adapter overheats and melts. Internal components short and cause a small fire. The adapter explodes, with capacitors bursting. The user receives an electric shock.

And one exploding adapter on TikTok can destroy your brand. It does not matter if you sold 10,000 units without incident. The one video with smoke and sparks is what people remember.

The way to protect your brand is straightforward. Specify a medical-grade **IEC 60601** adapter in the contract when appropriate, and test the received units rather than trusting paperwork alone. Request the certification documents and verify them with the issuing body. If a supplier offers to save five dollars per unit by “downgrading the adapter,” say no, and find a new supplier.

## The Worst Fraud Is on Paper

Now for the most dangerous deception of all: forged regulatory documents.

We have seen fake **FDA 510(k)** clearance letters created in Photoshop, using real clearance numbers from other companies’ devices. We have seen fake supplier cooperation contracts showing a relationship with a certified manufacturer that does not exist. We have seen mismatched model numbers, where the contract lists Model X but the device listing on Amazon is Model Y.

By some estimates, over **90%** of IPL hair removal devices sold on Amazon US use fake or misleading FDA documentation.

Why does this happen? Amazon’s initial document review is automated. A well-forged PDF can pass, for a while.

But enforcement eventually catches up. Amazon has teams dedicated to product-safety compliance. When they find discrepancies, the listing is removed, the seller account is suspended, funds are frozen, and in serious cases the matter can be referred for criminal prosecution.

We know of brands that achieved **$6 million USD** in annual sales on Amazon, built entirely on fake FDA documents. When Amazon audited them, the house of cards collapsed. Lawsuits followed. The founders now face potential jail time.

Let me be clear about the legal reality: selling an uncertified medical device, and IPL is a **Class II medical device** in the US, with forged clearance documents is not just a marketplace violation. It crosses into wire fraud and counterfeiting.

If you want to verify a real certification, go to the FDA **510(k)** database, search by applicant or device name, and confirm that the exact model number appears. If the supplier says “we have FDA clearance,” ask for the **510(k)** number and verify it yourself. If they refuse or keep making excuses, walk away.

## We Learned These Lessons From Clients

We did not learn these lessons from a textbook. We learned them from clients, sometimes after they had already been burned.

The Middle Eastern buyer who wanted a glowing replica was a wake-up call. We realized that many brands in the market were not selling hair-removal devices at all. They were selling light-up toys with pretty boxes.

The energy-inflating supplier showed up when a client’s device failed third-party lab testing. The display said **18 J/cm²**. The calibrated energy meter said **9 J/cm²**. Half the claimed energy.

The fake FDA documents surfaced when a client proudly sent us a “clearance letter.” We searched the FDA database. The number belonged to a different company, a different device, from ten years ago.

Each of these moments was frustrating. But they also taught us exactly what to look for, what to ask, and what to reject.

Today we have a zero-tolerance policy for these tricks. If a buyer asks for a fake replica, we say no. If a supplier offers inflated specs, we walk. If a certification cannot be verified, the deal is dead.

## What You Should Verify Before Signing Anything

Before you sign any contract or make any payment, here is what you should verify: energy output via an independent lab test report, not the supplier’s own; adapter certification, specifying medical-grade **IEC 60601** or certified commercial, with certificate copies; **FDA 510(k)** clearance, searched in the public database with a matching model number; **CE** mark, with the notified body number verified; **ISO 13485**, checking the certificate issuer against accredited registrars; a factory visit or live video call to see the production line and test equipment; and sample testing, which means ordering samples, testing energy output yourself, and using the device for weeks.

If a supplier refuses any of these checks, do not proceed. The cost of skipping verification is always higher than the cost of walking away.

## Why These Problems Keep Coming Back

The IPL niche is profitable. That is why dishonest suppliers and desperate brands keep trying to cut corners.

But the shortcuts, fake energy, dangerous adapters, forged certificates, all lead to the same destination: burned customers, banned accounts, and bankrupt businesses.

We have seen it happen. We have helped clients recover from it. And we have built our own processes to ensure it never happens to the brands we partner with.

If you are new to this industry, the learning curve is steep. The fake news, fake specs, and fake documents are everywhere.

You do not need a slogan to understand the point. In this category, the brands that survive are usually not the ones that move fastest. They are the ones that verify the details other people ignore.
