## Introduction

Last year, a friend of mine, let’s call her Sarah, walked into a venture capital meeting with a pitch deck that looked pretty solid. Her brand had six months of IPL sales, respectable margins, and a growing email list. She was not asking for a fortune. She just wanted enough capital to scale marketing and secure better manufacturing terms.

The partner listened politely, nodded at the right moments, and then said no. Not a harsh no. A polite, almost apologetic no. Sarah asked why. The partner hesitated, then gave three reasons almost off the cuff: low repeat purchase rate, total addressable market too small, and the fact that suppliers, not brands, hold the real leverage in this category.

That conversation stayed with me because it captures something strange about the IPL industry. Venture capitalists will not touch it with a ten-foot pole. Yet at the same time, no single brand has managed to build a monopoly. Not Philips. Not Braun. Not Silk'n. Not even Ulike, which holds a dominant position on Tmall but is still far from locking the category.

The market is profitable, growing, and full of brands. But it is also structurally stuck. Let me unpack what the VC meant, because those three reasons reveal more about IPL than most market reports do.

## Why the VC Said No

The first reason, low repeat purchase rate, is the simplest. IPL devices are durable goods. A customer buys one, uses it for eight to twelve weeks, achieves meaningful hair reduction, and may never buy another one again. Replacement lamp cartridges exist, but attachment rates are low. Compare that with SaaS, where revenue recurs every month, or with shampoo and razors, where customers restock constantly. VCs live for recurring revenue. IPL offers very little of it.

That alone might still be survivable if the market were massive. But the second reason cuts deeper. The global hair removal device market was valued at roughly **$858 million in 2025** and is projected to reach about **$1.73 billion by 2032**. That growth rate is respectable, but the absolute size is still modest. The entire category, including both IPL and laser, professional and home devices, is smaller than many single-brand skincare businesses. A market that tops out below two billion dollars globally does not look exciting to firms managing multi-billion-dollar funds.

The third reason is the one most founders underestimate. Manufacturing IPL devices requires real upfront work: injection molding tooling, PCB assembly, optical component sourcing, and regulatory clearance. But once a factory has already built those capabilities for one brand, producing for a second brand adds very little marginal cost. That flips the normal power dynamic. In many consumer categories, brands capture value and suppliers compete for their orders. In IPL, contract manufacturers often hold more leverage than the brands they serve. A founder can spend months developing what feels like a unique feature, only to watch the same factory offer a similar version to another client soon after.

So the VC passed. And Sarah walked away without a check.

## Why the Category Still Stays Crowded

Here is the twist. Even without venture capital, the IPL market remains crowded. Dozens of small and medium-sized brands compete aggressively, and none of them has built anything close to a monopoly. Even the companies that have been in the category for years remain constrained by the same structural forces.

That is what makes IPL such an unusual market. It is unattractive to big capital, but it also resists total domination by any single player.

## The Three-Tier Shape of the Market

Once you look at the category structure, the fragmentation starts to make sense.

At the top sit the global consumer electronics giants: Philips, Braun, and Panasonic. Together they hold a meaningful share of the market, but that share is spread across multiple technologies, geographies, and channels. More importantly, IPL is just one product line among many for them. They do not need to win the category outright. They only need to participate profitably. That means they are rarely motivated to invest at the level required to crush everyone else.

Below them are the D2C specialists such as RoseSkinCo, SmoothSkin, and Kenzzi. These brands have reached real scale without massive capital raises. They win through digital marketing, sharp positioning, and fast iteration. But none of them holds more than a low single-digit share globally. They are too large to be dismissed, but too small to dominate.

Then there is the third layer: legacy innovators like Silk'n, CosBeauty, and Ya-Man. Silk'n is especially important because of its historical role. Shimon Eckhouse helped commercialize foundational IPL technology in Israel in the 1990s, which gave Silk'n genuine first-mover credibility. Yet even that technological head start did not turn into global category control. In IPL, technological primacy does not automatically create lasting market power.

## China Shows the Fragmentation Most Clearly

If you want to see this fragmentation in its clearest form, look at China.

According to the 2025 Home IPL Industry White Paper, Ulike holds about **47% of Tmall's IPL market share**. That sounds dominant until you look more closely. JOVS holds roughly **11.25%**. Together, the top two brands control a little over half of one major sales channel. The remaining share is scattered across dozens of other brands.

That is not a monopoly. It is a fragmented market with a leader that is still vulnerable.

The failures of other large or technically credible players are even more revealing. Xiaomi tested the category through its ecosystem model, but its share remained marginal. Its real strengths, connected devices and recurring software engagement, do not translate well to IPL. Haier faced an even more basic problem: a home appliance brand trying to sell a beauty device created a trust gap. And Cyden, despite deep technology credentials and ownership of foundational IPL patents, struggled to gain traction in China because it lacked local brand recognition, strong platform positioning, and local consumer trust.

## Why No One Can Build a Monopoly

The answer is not a single barrier. It is a combination of reinforcing conditions that keep the market open.

One is the accessibility of manufacturing. The component ecosystem for IPL devices is mature and concentrated in China's Guangdong region. A brand with a relatively modest budget can hire an industrial designer, contract a factory, and pursue FDA clearance through the 510(k) pathway using an existing predicate device. That makes entry possible for thousands of entrepreneurs.

Another is the nature of distribution. IPL is sold primarily online, and online distribution favors agile, marketing-driven operators more than capital-heavy incumbents. A brand with a serious Meta ads budget can compete for attention in the same feed as Philips. The battlefield is much flatter than many people assume.

There is also the problem of technology commoditization. The core IPL stack, flash lamp, reflector, filter, capacitor bank, has been understood for decades. Improvements exist, including cooling systems, faster repetition, and better sensors, but no single brand owns a technological leap large enough to lock everyone else out. Consumers now compare energy density, cooling, treatment speed, and skin sensing as baseline features. These are closer to table stakes than moats.

And finally there is the supply chain itself. The concentration of IPL manufacturing in the Pearl River Delta means multiple brands are often built in overlapping factory networks using highly similar components. Even when a brand believes it has created something proprietary, the underlying capability often diffuses through the supply chain much faster than expected.

## What This Means for Founders

Put all of that together, and the paradox stops looking like a bug. The same forces that keep big capital away also keep the category open.

That is why someone like Sarah can fail to raise venture funding and still have a viable business. She does not need to beat Philips globally. She needs a profitable customer acquisition model, a credible product, and enough operational discipline to survive in a fragmented field. The category is hard to monopolize, but that also makes it surprisingly survivable for disciplined smaller brands.

This is the strange comfort of IPL. No one is likely to eat your lunch overnight, but you are also unlikely to build the kind of winner-take-all company that venture capital wants.

## The More Interesting Question

The real question is no longer why no one dominates IPL. The more interesting question is what would actually have to change for a true monopoly to emerge.

Would it take a home-use technology that delivers genuinely permanent results rather than long-term reduction? Would it take a recurring-revenue model that turns a one-time hardware purchase into an ongoing relationship? Would it take a regulatory shift that makes entry dramatically more expensive and wipes out most smaller competitors?

None of those conditions clearly exist today. So the category remains where Sarah found it: unattractive to venture capital, open to smaller operators, and still structurally resistant to monopoly. The next useful question is not whether IPL is fragmented. It is which change, technological, regulatory, or economic, would be powerful enough to end that fragmentation.
